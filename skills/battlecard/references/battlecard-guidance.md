# Battlecard Writing Guidance

Reference this file for detailed advice on writing each section of a competitive battlecard well.

---

## Section 1 & 2: Competitor Overview & Positioning

**Goal:** Give a new rep just enough context to understand who this competitor is and what story they tell.

**Do:**
- Describe their target customer (not just their product)
- Capture the *emotional* narrative they sell — e.g. "They position as the enterprise-safe choice" or "They lead with speed and simplicity for SMBs"
- Note their primary go-to-market motion (PLG, direct enterprise, channel)

**Don't:**
- Copy-paste their marketing tagline as if it's analysis
- Write more than 4–5 sentences — reps won't read it

**Sources to check:**
- Competitor homepage headline and subhead
- Their "About" or "Why us" page
- G2 / Capterra / Gartner reviews (how customers describe them)
- Recent press releases or funding announcements

---

## Section 3: Where We Win

**Goal:** Give reps 3–5 crisp, defensible differentiators tied to customer outcomes.

**Do:**
- Anchor each differentiator to a buyer pain point, not a feature
- Use comparative language: "Unlike [competitor], we..." or "The only solution that..."
- Prioritize differentiators that come up in actual deals

**Don't:**
- List features without explaining why they matter
- Include things that are table-stakes for the category
- Use vague claims ("more powerful", "better UX") without specifics

**Example (bad):** "We have better reporting"
**Example (good):** "Our reporting is real-time and doesn't require a BI team to configure — buyers running lean ops teams consistently flag this as a deciding factor"

---

## Section 4: Where They Win Against Us

**Goal:** Prepare reps for the hard conversations so they're not caught off guard.

**Do:**
- Be honest — reps will discover this in deals regardless
- Provide a reframe or neutralizing response for each weakness
- Distinguish between "genuinely weaker" vs. "different tradeoff"

**Reframe patterns:**
- Depth vs. breadth: "They go deeper on X, but that's because they don't do Y and Z — which our buyers need"
- Legacy vs. modern: "They've been around longer, which means more technical debt and slower innovation"
- Price: "They are cheaper, but [TCO argument / switching cost / missing capability]"

---

## Section 5: Objections & Responses

**Goal:** A rapid-response reference for the most common competitive objections.

**Format:** Write objections as the buyer would actually say them — not sanitized versions.

**Response structure (FEEL / FELT / FOUND works well):**
- Acknowledge: "I hear that — a lot of customers have looked at both"
- Reframe: "What they've found is..."
- Redirect: "Can I ask what's driving that concern specifically?"

**Common objection categories to cover:**
- Price ("They're cheaper")
- Features ("They have X and you don't")
- Brand/trust ("They're more established")
- Switching cost ("We already use their other products")
- Reference ("Someone recommended them")

---

## Section 6: Landmine Questions

**Goal:** Questions a rep can ask in discovery that surface problems the competitor can't solve — without ever mentioning the competitor's name.

**Principles:**
- Frame as genuine curiosity, not gotcha
- Target known weaknesses in the competitor's product or model
- Should feel natural in a discovery call, not scripted

**Examples of good landmine questions:**
- "How important is it that your team can configure this without involving engineering?" (targets complex setup)
- "Do you have a dedicated admin who owns this tool, or does it need to be self-serve?" (targets onboarding complexity)
- "What does your data residency or compliance requirement look like?" (targets missing certifications)
- "How quickly do you typically need to iterate on workflows as your process changes?" (targets rigid/legacy architecture)

---

## Section 7: Proof Points & Win Stories

**Goal:** Give reps a story to tell when the buyer says "do you have customers like us who switched from [competitor]?"

**Sources:**
- Win/loss call notes from sales
- Customer success stories or case studies
- Testimonials on G2 or the website that mention competitors
- Direct asks to sales team: "What's the best competitive win story you have vs. X?"

**Format guidance:**
- Keep to 1–2 sentences per story
- Include: industry, the problem, the outcome — not just "they chose us"
- Anonymize if the customer hasn't approved public reference

**If no stories exist yet:** Note the gap explicitly in the battlecard and flag it to the PMM. Don't invent stories.

---

## Section 8: Quick Reference Card

**Goal:** Something a rep can mentally review in the 2 minutes before jumping on a call where this competitor is in play.

**Keep it to:**
- Their one-liner pitch
- Your one-liner counter
- The single best differentiator to lead with in that competitive context
- One landmine question to always ask

This should be answerable from memory after reading the full battlecard once.
