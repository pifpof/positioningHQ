# Interview Guide — Product Launch Skill

Detailed guidance for conducting the founder interview well.
Read this alongside the interview protocol in SKILL.md.

---

## Principles

**Feel like a conversation, not a questionnaire.**
Don't fire all questions in a block. Ask one or two, listen, then follow up before moving to the next topic. A founder who feels heard gives richer answers.

**The best answers come from "tell me more."**
When a founder gives a vague answer, the instinct is to move on. Don't. Probe with:
- *"Can you give me a specific example of that?"*
- *"What does that look like in practice?"*
- *"When you say [X], what do you mean exactly?"*
- *"How often does that happen?"*

**Specificity is everything.**
Vague inputs produce vague outputs. If a founder says "our customers are busy professionals," push for: what kind of professional, in what industry, at what company size, dealing with what specific frustration. The more specific the interview, the more useful the launch plan.

**Listen for what they don't say.**
Founders often skip over the problem because they're focused on the solution. If they jump straight to features, slow them down:
- *"Before you tell me what it does — help me understand the problem it's solving. What were customers doing before this?"*

---

## Red Flags to Probe Deeper

If you hear any of the following in the interview, it signals a gap that will cause problems in the launch plan. Flag it gently and explore it before moving on.

| What they say | What it might mean | How to probe |
|---|---|---|
| "Everyone is our customer" | No clear ICP — will dilute messaging | "If you could only sell to one type of person in the first 90 days, who would it be?" |
| "It's better than everything else" | No specific differentiation | "Better in what specific way? What's the one thing you do that no one else does?" |
| "We'll use social media and content" | No real channel plan | "Do you have an existing audience on those channels? How many followers or subscribers?" |
| "We want to go viral" | Unrealistic expectation | "What's the mechanism for that? What would make someone share this with a colleague?" |
| "Success is a lot of signups" | No outcome-tied metric | "What happens after signup? What does a successful user do in their first week?" |
| "We don't really have competitors" | Hasn't thought about alternatives | "What are customers doing today to solve this problem without you?" |
| "We're launching in 2 weeks" | Very tight timeline | "What's driving that date? Is it fixed or flexible?" |

## When to End the Interview

You have enough to proceed when you can confidently answer all of the following:

- [ ] What is the product and what problem does it solve?
- [ ] Who specifically is the target customer?
- [ ] What is the single most important differentiator?
- [ ] What does success look like at 30 days?
- [ ] What channels and assets does the founder have available?
- [ ] Are there any significant constraints (budget, team, deadline)?

If any of these are still unclear after 20 exchanges, name it directly:
> *"Before I move on — I want to make sure I understand [X]. Can you help me with that?"*

## Handling Common Founder Patterns

**The visionary who talks in abstractions**
Some founders describe their product in terms of grand mission rather than specific function. Bring them back to earth:
- *"That's a compelling vision. Help me understand what a customer does differently on day one with this product versus without it."*

**The engineer who leads with features**
Technical founders often describe what the product does without explaining why it matters:
- *"That's a really interesting capability. Who specifically benefits from that, and what were they doing before?"*

**The founder who undersells**
Some founders are so used to caveating that their answers are full of "it kind of does..." and "in some cases...":
- *"Let me ask you this differently — if a customer asks 'why should I use this?' what's your best answer?"*

**The founder who's done this before**
Experienced founders may have strong opinions about channels and messaging already. Don't fight it — incorporate their instincts but pressure-test assumptions:
- *"That makes sense. What evidence do you have that that channel will reach this specific customer?"*

## After the Interview — Synthesis Notes

Before writing the strategy checkpoint summary, mentally answer:

1. **What's the sharpest one-line description of this product?**
2. **Who is the most specific version of the target customer?**
3. **What's the honest competitive advantage — not the aspirational one?**
4. **What's the most realistic launch scenario given their constraints?**
5. **Where are the gaps that could undermine the launch if not addressed?**

The strategy checkpoint is your chance to surface the gaps before they become problems in the output.
