# Output Specifications — Product Launch Skill

Detailed instructions for generating each of the six launch output files.
All files are markdown. Each must be self-contained and usable without reading the others.

---

## 1. Messaging Narrative
**File:** `launch-messaging-narrative.md`
**Purpose:** The strategic messaging foundation. Everything else pulls from this.

### Sections to include:

**Positioning Statement** (internal use, not marketing copy)
Use this structure:
> For [target customer] who [has this problem], [product name] is a [category] that [key benefit]. Unlike [main alternative], we [key differentiator].

**The One-Line Pitch**
The sharpest, most memorable version of what this product does. Written for a customer, not an investor. No jargon. Should survive being said out loud.

**The Problem We're Solving**
2–3 paragraphs describing the customer's world *before* this product. Written from the customer's perspective. What are they frustrated by? What are they doing instead? What's the cost of that workaround?

**Why Now**
1 paragraph on why this problem is particularly acute or why this solution is possible now. Market timing, technology shift, regulatory change, or behavioural shift. If there's no strong "why now," say so and suggest angles to develop.

**The Three Messages That Matter**
The three most important things a customer should understand about this product. Each message should:
- Be one sentence
- Be tied to a customer outcome, not a feature
- Be differentiated from what competitors say

**Proof Points**
Evidence that backs up each of the three messages. Pull from what the founder shared. If proof is thin or missing, flag it explicitly:
> ⚠️ *This message needs supporting evidence — consider: early customer quotes, beta results, relevant data, or a relevant analogy.*

**What We Are Not**
1–2 sentences on what this product deliberately doesn't do or who it's not for. Helps sales and marketing avoid attracting the wrong customers.

## 2. GTM Channel Plan
**File:** `launch-gtm-channel-plan.md`
**Purpose:** A realistic, prioritised plan for how to reach the target customer at launch.

### Approach
Present 4–6 channel options relevant to the founder's audience, budget, and existing assets. For each, give an honest assessment — do not recommend channels they can't execute. Let the founder choose their priorities.

### Structure:

**Launch Tier Summary**
One paragraph setting realistic expectations based on what the founder told you about their budget, team, and existing audience. If they're starting from zero with a small team, say that clearly and calibrate accordingly.

**Recommended Channel Options**

For each channel (4–6 total), include:

| Field | Content |
|---|---|
| Channel | Name |
| Best for | What this channel is good at |
| Effort | Low / Medium / High |
| Cost | Free / Low / Medium / High |
| Timeline to results | Days / Weeks / Months |
| Relevance for this launch | Why it does or doesn't fit based on what the founder told you |
| Suggested first action | One concrete thing to do this week |

Channel options to consider (select the most relevant based on the interview):
- Existing email list or waitlist
- Product Hunt / launch platforms
- LinkedIn (organic or paid)
- Content / SEO
- PR / press outreach
- Community channels (Slack, Reddit, Discord, forums)
- Partnerships or co-marketing
- Paid social (Meta, LinkedIn ads)
- Direct outreach / sales-led
- Existing customer expansion / referral

**Launch Week Sequence**
A day-by-day or week-by-week suggested sequence for the first 2 weeks. Concrete and actionable.

**What to Deprioritise**
Channels worth skipping for this launch and why. Helps the founder avoid spreading thin.

## 3. Success Metrics
**File:** `launch-success-metrics.md`
**Purpose:** A clear, specific measurement framework so the team knows if the launch worked.

### Structure:

**North Star Metric**
The single most important number that defines launch success. Should be a lagging indicator tied to business outcome (not vanity metrics). Example: "Activated trial users in week 1" not "website visits."

**30-Day Targets**

A table with 5–7 specific, measurable targets:

| Metric | Target | Why It Matters | How to Measure |
|---|---|---|---|
| [metric] | [number] | [rationale] | [tool or method] |

Calibrate targets to what the founder told you about their starting point. If they have no existing audience, a target of 10,000 signups is not realistic. If targets can't be set without more information, explain what baseline data is needed first.

**90-Day Targets**
3–4 metrics that indicate whether the launch has translated into sustained traction. These should be different from 30-day metrics — they should show retention, expansion, or compounding growth.

**Leading Indicators to Watch Weekly**
3–5 early signals that predict whether you're on track before the 30-day results land. Example: trial-to-activation rate, time-to-first-value, inbound demo requests.

**What Not to Measure**
A short list of vanity metrics to deprioritise — things that feel good to track but don't actually tell you if the launch worked.

**Review Cadence**
Suggested rhythm for reviewing launch metrics: daily in week 1, weekly in weeks 2–4, monthly thereafter.

## 4. Sales Enablement Brief
**File:** `launch-sales-enablement-brief.md`
**Purpose:** Everything a sales rep or founder needs to sell this product confidently from day one. Standalone document.

### Structure:

**What We're Launching** (2–3 sentences)
Plain language. No jargon. What it is, who it's for, and what it does.

**The Problem It Solves**
Written in customer language, mirrored from the messaging narrative.

**Who to Sell To**
- Primary buyer persona: role, responsibilities, what they care about
- Secondary personas if relevant
- Who to avoid (wrong-fit customers)

**The Pitch in 60 Seconds**
A structured talk track the rep can use in the first minute of any conversation. Should include: the problem, the solution, the key differentiator, and a question to open discovery.

**Key Value Props by Persona**
For each relevant persona, the 2–3 things they care most about. Pulled from the messaging narrative and tailored to their priorities.

**Discovery Questions to Ask**
8–10 questions that surface the pain this product solves. Tied directly to the problem statement.

**Common Objections and Responses**
5–7 objections with suggested responses. Structure: Acknowledge → Reframe → Redirect.

**Competitive Positioning**
How to position against the main alternatives the founder identified. Keep it brief — 2–3 sentences per alternative.

**Proof Points**
Customer quotes, beta results, or data points to use in sales conversations. Flag any that are missing.

**What Not to Promise**
Capabilities or use cases the product doesn't support yet. Prevents reps from overselling.

## 5. Internal FAQ
**File:** `launch-internal-faq.md`
**Purpose:** Answers the questions every employee, advisor, or partner will ask. Keeps the whole company aligned on the launch narrative.

### Structure:

**About the Product**
- What are we launching?
- Who is it for?
- What problem does it solve?
- How is it different from what's already out there?
- What does it not do?

**About the Customer**
- Who is the target customer?
- What does the buying process look like?
- What does a good-fit customer look like? What does a bad-fit look like?

**About the Launch**
- When are we launching?
- What channels are we using?
- What's the launch goal?
- What does success look like?

**About the Competition**
- Who are the main competitors?
- How do we position against them?
- What do we say if a customer asks about [main competitor]?

**What to Do If...**
- A customer asks a question you can't answer → [escalation path]
- You hear something in the market that seems relevant → [feedback path]
- A journalist or analyst asks about the launch → [who to direct them to]

**Key Messages to Use (and Avoid)**
A short list of approved language and phrases to avoid. Keeps messaging consistent across the company.

## 6. Launch Announcement
**File:** `launch-announcement.md`
**Purpose:** The external-facing announcement. Written for the primary channel the founder selected — adapt the tone accordingly (email, blog post, or social).

### Generate three versions:

**Version 1 — Email announcement**
For existing customers, waitlist, or subscribers. Tone: warm, direct, personal. Structure:
- Subject line (3 options)
- Opening line that earns attention
- The problem (1 short paragraph)
- The solution and what's new (2–3 short paragraphs)
- The key benefit in one sentence
- A single, clear CTA
- Sign-off

**Version 2 — Blog post / long-form announcement**
For website, Medium, or content channels. Tone: authoritative, story-driven. Structure:
- Headline (3 options)
- Opening hook — the problem or the insight
- The story of why we built this
- What it does and who it's for
- Early results or proof (if available)
- How to get started
- CTA

**Version 3 — Short social post**
For LinkedIn or Twitter/X. 3–4 sentences maximum. Punchy. Should stand alone without reading the blog post. Include 3 versions with slightly different angles (problem-led, outcome-led, story-led).

### Tone guidance
Pull tone from what the founder communicated in the interview. A technical B2B tool for enterprise RevOps teams sounds different from a consumer productivity app. Write the announcement in the voice that fits the product and audience — do not default to generic startup announcement language.

## 7. Positioning Update Recommendation
**File:** `positioning-update-recommendation.md`
**Phase:** 4 (Launch impact assessment — generated immediately after Phase 3)
**Purpose:** A structured handoff document for the playbook-update skill. Generated from the founder interview and launch messaging — no post-launch results required. Tells the playbook-update skill exactly what to change, at what depth, and what to leave alone. Must be precise enough to act on without follow-up questions.

**Only generate this file for Level 1, 2, or 3 impact launches.** Do not generate for Level 0 (incremental).

---

### Section 1 — Impact Summary

**Launch:** [Product or feature name]
**Date:** [Launch date or planned date]
**Assessment basis:** [What this assessment is based on — e.g. "founder interview and launch messaging narrative generated in this session"]
**Overall impact level:** [Level 1 — Additive / Level 2 — Evolutionary / Level 3 — Transformational]

**Impact level rationale** (2–3 sentences):
Explain which dimensions changed and at what degree, and what that means for the positioning playbook overall. Be direct — this is read by a skill, not a human audience.

---

### Section 2 — Dimension-by-Dimension Assessment

For each of the five dimensions, state the change degree and provide the reasoning. This is the analytical core of the document — be specific.

**ICP**
- Change degree: [None / Partial / Full]
- Current ICP (from baseline): [Summarise]
- What changed and why: [Specific reasoning from interview]
- Recommended new ICP language: [Draft the exact language the playbook should use — or "no change"]

**Market Segment**
- Change degree: [None / Partial / Full]
- Current segment (from baseline): [Summarise]
- What changed and why: [Specific reasoning]
- Recommended update: [Exact language or "no change"]

**Positioning and Category**
- Change degree: [None / Partial / Full]
- Current positioning (from baseline): [Summarise]
- What changed and why: [Specific reasoning]
- Recommended update: [Exact language or "no change"]

**Value Drivers and Key Messages**
- Change degree: [None / Partial / Full]
- Current key messages (from baseline): [List them]
- What changed and why: [Specific reasoning]
- Recommended new messages: [Draft exact replacements or additions, or "no change"]

**Differentiation and Brand Story**
- Change degree: [None / Partial / Full]
- Current differentiation story (from baseline): [Summarise]
- What changed and why: [Specific reasoning]
- Recommended update: [Exact language or "no change"]

---

### Section 3 — Prioritised Change List

A flat list of every recommended change, ordered by priority. Scaled to impact level:
- Level 1: 1–3 changes (additions and refinements only)
- Level 2: 3–6 changes (revisions with specific new language)
- Level 3: Full rewrite — list what to carry forward and what to replace entirely

For each change:

---
**Change [N]: [Short label]**
- **Playbook section:** [ICP / Market Segment / Positioning / Key Messages / Differentiation / Proof Points / Competitive Positioning / Brand Story]
- **Current text:** [Quote or paraphrase from existing playbook — or "section does not exist yet"]
- **Recommended text:** [Exactly what it should say — write the replacement, not a description of what to write]
- **Basis:** [What from the interview or launch messaging supports this — be specific]
- **Confidence:** [High / Medium / Low] — [one sentence explanation]
- **Priority:** [Must change / Should change / Consider changing]

---

### Section 4 — What to Leave Unchanged

List every positioning element this launch *confirms* should stay the same. This prevents the playbook-update skill from over-editing.

- **[Element]:** [Why it's confirmed — what in the interview or messaging supports keeping it]

For a Level 3 launch, this section may be short. State clearly if the majority of the playbook should be treated as superseded.

---

### Section 5 — Handoff Note for Playbook-Update Skill

A direct instruction to the playbook-update skill. Include:
- The impact level and what it means for how aggressively to apply changes
- Which changes are high-confidence and should be applied directly
- Which changes are medium-confidence and should be flagged as suggested revisions for the founder to confirm
- Any sections of the playbook the skill should not touch
- Whether this is an update to an existing playbook or the establishment of a first playbook from scratch

Example for a Level 2 launch:
> *"This is a Level 2 — Evolutionary update to the positioning playbook. Apply all 'Must change' items directly. Flag 'Should change' items as suggested revisions. Do not alter the competitive positioning section — it was confirmed as accurate in the interview. The ICP update is the highest priority change and should be reflected consistently across all affected sections."*

Example for a Level 3 launch:
> *"This is a Level 3 — Transformational update. Treat the existing playbook as a starting point only. The brand story, ICP, and key messages should be fully rewritten using the recommended language in Section 3. Carry forward the proof points listed in Section 4 — they remain valid. Flag the full rewrite clearly for founder review before publishing."*
